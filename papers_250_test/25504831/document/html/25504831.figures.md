## Figure 1

Figure 1 , 448 HIV-infected pregnant women received prenatal care at the HFSE institution between 4/2010 and 10/2012. Within this population, 232 (52%) fulfilled Ministry of Health criteria for HIV genotypic susceptibility testing and 231 had plasma specimens assayed for this purpose. Table 1 demonstrates the breakdown of the demographics and characteristics of our study population. Sixty-four percent of women were between 18 and 30 years of age, and over half of the cohort had been diagnosed before the current pregnancy. The vast majority of patients fulfilled Brazilian criteria for low socio-economic status and were of African heritage, which mirrors the social profile of the HIV epidemic in pregnant women in Rio de Janeiro. Forty-six percent of patients had not completed primary school and 71% used tobacco during pregnancy. Among 231 eligible subjects, 105 patients were diagnosed with HIV infection prior to entering prenatal care at HFSE, and of these, 56 had previous exposure to cART. This includes three women who were perinatally infected themselves and had been on cART for many years. The mean CD4 cell count at study entry was 406 cells/mm 3 (95% CI: 373–438 cells/mm 3 ) and the mean viral load 24,394 copies/ml (95% CI: 18,275–30,513 copies/ml). The majority of women initiated cART during the second trimester of pregnancy, the most common first line regimen being zidovudine (ZDV) + lamivudine (3TC) + lopinavir/ritonavir (LPV/RTV) ( n = 175). By 34 weeks gestation, 147/231 women (64%) achieved an undetectable viral load (<400 copies/ml); 48/231 subjects (20.8%) had levels above 400 copies/ml plasma and 36/231 subjects (16%) did not have a viral load result at 34 weeks, either because they did not come for that visit or because of premature delivery.
